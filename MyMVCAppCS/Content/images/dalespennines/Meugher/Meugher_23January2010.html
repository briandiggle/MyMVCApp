Meugher from Middlesmoor, 23 January 2010

1. 55 minutes only from Blackmoor Court, the day started much better than promised. Parked opposite the pub where the landlord made me Egg and Chips for lunch and stopped to chat with me (his only customer) a few years back. This friendliness was a feature today as well.

2. Little changed since my last visit, the converted wesleyan chapel. The distinctive "farm" smell greeted the visitor on approach, the first building on the left being occupied with cows...

3. First section of the walk was on the Nidderdale Way. A great start in sunshine with the first sign of the snow which would be a big part of the walk.

4. 

5. View from Stean Pasture - Middlesmoor middle right, Stean village below. Rain Stang above.

6. Near Moor House, the FP was unclear. That together with barking dogs persuaded me to go round heading a little up Whit Beck. Each farm passed had its dogs, and I had the feeling the my passing was well noted and was looking forward to the anonymity of the high moors. This was the first encounter with deep snow of the day. This snow had drifted to around 2 or 3 feet deep and was nicely frozen on  top- I didnt fall through (on this occasion).

This section also introduced me to another feature of this walk- rabbits dead or alive. Three rabbits were lying dead together on the ground. I wondered why? Later I found out!

7. Approaching the first "shooting box" . This seems to be a nice feature of this area - shooting hut providing an escape from the elements left open for the occasional walker to enjoy.

8. Inside the shooting box. Enjoyed cuppa rather than booze as shown! Two new skills learnt today - early on was how to make a nice flask of tea by stewing "in situ". The other was how( not) to fit gaiters. I was surprised to realise that like crampons, gaiters are also secured by running an attachment under the instep. I couln't work out what the second set of laces was, dispensed with them. Then I found that each time my foot went into the deep snow, the gaiter was forced over the top of the boot and a little snow entered over the top. By the end of the walk, despite having gaiters on, my feet and boots were soaked.

9.

10. First shooting box looking towards Middlesmoor. Just as I was leaving, a farmer was approaching on a quad. I was sure he had seen me shutting the door and thought I'd get told off. In fact he was friendly and disappeared up the track with two dogs.

11. The cloud level was around 650 meters all day - Great Whernside was in mist. Most of the vista had  a kind of "widescreen" look!

12. It was around here between the two "shooting boxes" that the farmer came back with his two dogs. He stopped and switch off the quad and was very chatty. One of his first questions was "Where are you from"? Leeds probably didnt seem to surprise him or put him off. I further showed my "offcomer" nature by saying I was off "Mewer". "Ah yes Mewfer"  I got to demontrate my local knowledge late in the day as the first farm on the way down was interested in where I had been - "Mewfer" I replied! He was giving his two dogs a little run out hunting rabbits. I showed by ignorance asking if the dead animal in the cage on the quad was a hare. "Hares disappeared 25 years ago - no, it 's a rabbit". "They need thinning out". I could understand what he meant because they did seem everywhere. He told also the story of how after the winter of 1947 it is said that snow was collected from Meugher and taken to the Nidderdale Show in September. He also said the little hump near us called "Stock Ridge" was in a national paper a couple of year back as the geographical centre of Britain.

13. Found this lying on the snow by the track - another kill- is it a baby shrew?

14. Much more snow now approaching the second shooting box

15. Looking over to Little Whernside, which stayed just below the cloud ceiling all day.

16. Rain stang to the right

17. The second higher "shooting box" and the last homely house in the West. This really did fell like the edge of the wild. From here, there is no road in sight only miles and miles of high moorland and mountain. Nowhere in the Lakes feels like this.

18. The second shooting box where I had lunch. Looking towards Great Haw in the centre.

19. It was only at this stage that the main objective for the day comes into view, and I was surprised with how well defined the hill was. Lots of deep snow stood between me and the OS pillar on top. Alot of it was the worst stuff - deep but with a poorly frozen surface so that I one step I would be on top, the next a foot down as the surface collapsed. Hard work!

20. The most dramatic moment of the day came when this cloud inversion appeared as altitude was gained and the view West was revealed. I assumed this was Whernside?

21. 

22. This was the point at which the track turned away from Meugher and the hardest section of the walk began. Walking on the western facing slopes the snow was wet and unstable, but still deep. I had 600m to cross to the summit. At times I was hopping between grass tussocks peeping out.

39. Descending off the Eastern flank of Meugher, although there was more snow, it was more stable and easier to walk on. At times I was aware of walking over a concealed stream and hoped I wouldnt end up in it!

40. Had to cross this stream defile and felt a little of the fear mountaineers must feel when crossing ice-fields with hidden crevasses!

41. The return route following Straight Stean Beck. Another 2 Km section of the West facing snow lay ahead making the going very hard work

42. At last, rejoining the track and easier progress.
