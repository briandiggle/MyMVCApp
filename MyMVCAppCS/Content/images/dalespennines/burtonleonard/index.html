Wormald Green to South Stainley via Burton Leonard<

22 April 2005

14Km - Sunny

Running out of walks in new territory from the 36 bus route,
this was the obvious one with the extra interest of visiting
Burton Leonard. Did a bit of research on Burton Leonard Cricket
Club and found their website. A section on club history included
a photo of the 1950 team with Dad in the front row!

Stepped off at wormald green and had a look at the first
historical marker - the George Inn pub where the Diggles had their
golden? wedding. It is now up for sale at auction in June.
First part of the walk followed a footpath over fields and
arriving at the perfect place to spy out old ground - the
score board of the cricket club near Peter Lane.

The "Monkton Mains" farm had redirected the footpath
without any indication away from the farm. So I had decided
to get out of view of the farm buildings (had already met
the farmer) and "off road" back onto the footpath position
using the GPS. It turned out that the re-routed footpath
followed the exact position I was going to take! Sat
down for a coffee and listened to the peace of the
countryside. How I need this space. I have been unable to
get any time to unwind since christmas with Linda not
being well, the operation, and all my church responsibilities.

I was going to use the time to re-think what I am
currently doing because it has become clear to me that its
too much at the moment to carry. Talking to Pete during the
week was great as it became clear to both of us that
I obviously need to cut stuff out or I will go under. The
list of church responsibilities at present - elder, lead
worship once a month, lead community outreach group, co-lead
housegroup. Add to this the stress of Linda not being in
a good place with the operation and the stress of all
the issues around adoption. I could do all the church if
things were good at home- but they-re not.

Most of the day I just walked slowly, taking in the
surroundings. I was walking in a gently rolling
landscape of fields descending to the Vale of York around
the middle of the day, then rising back up.

More and more I can appreciate walks such as this without
the grandeur of mountain scenery. I took the book on wild
flowers I bought in February at York with the aim of
identifying my first wild flower.

Excitiment mounted as I approached the outskirts of Burton
Leonard. I suddenly came upon the score-board building of
BLCC. Taking a leak round the back I wondered if my Dad
had ever peed in the same spot?!

My next task was to spy out the Wrays house on Peter Lane,
take some photos and move on un-noticed. The old scrap
yard at the edge of their house has been sold and three
houses have been built on the site, the nearest having
been built right up against the boundary. I walked
out into the central green space of the village. The
next ritual was to buy something at the village store
and sit in the shelter opposite with a coffee and watch
the village go by.

Next stop was the church opposite where Ladbroke House used
to be. Lost of old trees including a baby yew taken , a
cutting from the 2000 year old Buxted yew. It is one of 7000 yews
distributed in Autumn 1999 from cuttings of ancient yews to mark the
millenium. I actually recognised two mature Scots Pines nearest
to Ladbroke House and remembered looking out of the sitting room
window at their straight trunks.

Last mandatory stop on memory lane was to stand in the
middle of the little close crammed with houses on the
site of the old apple orchard. Round the back of the
larger house built on the actual Ladbroke House site
there still seems to be some of the old ramshackle
garden and trees.

Went a short way up mill lane and then took the footpath
on the right leading on the edge of new house building to
straight lane. Here I picked up a glossy yellow
wild flower with its foliage for later indentification.
This turned out to be a Lesser Celandile or Pile Wort!
I felt a sense of jubilation at having begun to discover
a rich new world. Road side verges are no longer places
to ignore but rich wild habitats where wild flowers have
can grow undisturbed away from the managed farm fields.

Onward to Red Hills Lane, then taking the footpath to
the right towards moor farm. This is one of those farms
where the location of footpaths has disappeared. I had
to walk straight across a sown field (not too bad at this
time of year, the crop only knee high). When arriving at
the farm buildings the route of the FP was blocked so had
to tramp round the back.

Now headed on the FP towards foster flatts farm looking for
a place to stop for lunch. There is an area of managed
grouse-rearing woodland between these two farms. I stopped
in a clearing-area to the left of the FP with some old
woodcutting equipment. This was a much nicer way to take
lunch than in some un-friendly dingy pub- warm, quiet,
private with just the odd butterfly and squirrel for
company.

Off to foster flatts farm and strode boldly throught
the middle of the farm buildings though afterwards noticed
that the FP actually skirted them on the L.
On the track to the farm I noticed a large ash tree with
some of last year's keys so grabbed hold of a set. At this
point the farmer passed in his car looking rather bemused!

At the junction with Boroughbridge road, turned left towards
Roecliffe moor and explored an area of Beech woodland with
blue-bells just coming towards their peak. This path took
me to Copgrove then took the FP near the bridge into
Mountgarret estates land. I noticed this landowner has put
alot of effort into tree-planting. Many individual trees,
often horse-chestnut have been planted along with whole
new woodland areas.

The path then followed the route of the beck and joins the
Ripon Rowel. The point at which the Rowel turns right there
is a T junction with a signpost and old boundary stone. I
placed a marker next to the E side of this stone as pretty
much the highest point of the day.

Just before I got to Stainley hall farm I notice to the R
of the FP an small patch of natural woodland. I peered
with and saw a magical scene like the Shire in the Spring-
a carpet of woodland flowers of all types. Couldnt resist
entering and standing in the stillness taking in this
wonderful scene. It had a feeling of natural enchantment.

The last excitement of the day was the only geocache in this
area at SE 30857 62992. Found it within seconds and left my
now customary calling card - Matt 13:44
