Ripley to Ripon

20 December 2004

Caught the 36 in chapel Allerton just after a bright dawn.
By the time I'd arrived at Ripley (9:30) it was raining
and the weather had closed in. The forecast was clearing
up to sunny intervals in the afternoon, so I had some hope.

A good excuse to christen the overtrousers I needed on
Ben More anyway and found they kept me nice and warm so
left them on throughout the day.

As it was a Saturday, thought I would avoid main paths
and so avoided the Nidderdale Way route N out of Ripley
and followed the FP to Newton Beck which was guarded by
the herd of cows. The, thanks to the Ramblers, followed
the Permissive footpath skirting Sir Henry Wood. Here I
had my first of 3 encourters with people - a local old
gent with a 'Big Michael Wray' accent walking th'dog.
Advised me that there were 2 pubs in Markington doing
food.

Then met up with the Nidderdale Way, passed by one
runner and his dog. Here the first rays of sunshine
appeared. I left a marker at the highest point of the
mornings walk at SE285623 at RH turn in the footpath
as it first meets Cayton Gill Wood. It is underneath
the post with FP marker (on the right, looking N) at the turn.

Thinking about these markers, they are reminders for
me to choose the way of the cross, as the stones point
E. They remind me to "set my face to Jerusalem" as Jesus
did.

Just past this point, there is a nice viewpoint looking
across the Vale of York to the North York Moors.

Steeply down towards Cayton Gill Farm and a meeting with
some bored horses. Then head NW along a nice enclosed
valley area. The T junction marks the intersection with
the Ripley - South Stainley walk I did a while back. I
follow this until at High Cayton take the FP heading
towards Hincks Hall. Stopping for a coffee hard boiled
egg at the second wall I meet, I realise I am ahead of
schedule for 12noon at Markington.

So skip across to the Bridleway leading to Horse Hill Wood. The sound of Dogs
barking gets closer and closer and then I come across
a rather shabby looking kennels with no-one about. Dogs
with all types of bark are holding a competition. I decide
to see if I can add my bark in situ. There is a stunned
silence after my first bark. They start again. I bark.
Again a stunned silence! Next strange sight is a man with
a flag at the edge of the wood near Hob Green Hotel, waving
it at the wood for no apparent reason. Then remembering my
talk with the landlord at Middlesmoor, realise this guy is
probably a 'beater'.

This is shortly followed by third strange sight of quick
succession - a herd of cows chasing two deer down a field.
Presumable, the deer had breached the cows fundamental
rights to graze the grass of their field. So this turned out
to be an interesting diversion. It finished in farce however
as the 'short-cut' to Markington via FP to the W end of the
village ended in a farmyard covered in 3 inch deep 'swill'.
Took some brushing down to make myself presentable for the
pub.

Turns out that only one pub was offering lunch, and that only
at weekends- so I was now glad I didnt do the walk yesterday.
The local was almost empty except for a group of locals.

Back out for section 2 of the walk and took Westerns Lane
until the Bridleway on the right. There were now no signs until
I reached Straight Lane. Ingerthorpe Grange (Yorkshire riding
centre) was difficult to navigate. Suffice to say I had explored
fairly well by the time I got out. Just as I was finally figuring out
what track to take out of the complex, a dog began to get
quite interested in my loitering behaviour and began barking
menacingly. It was a case of walking resolutely in the right
direction, maintaining my stride while the dog barked in front
of me. Things then got more desperate when its friend joined
the fun! I continued and after 30 seconds or so of me ignoring
them, they seemed to decide that I was OK and then turned
into my companions, running around me and ahead of me on
the track as if showing the way.

The limit of their domain was reached as we got to the little
pond on the way to Ingerthorpe Hall, and they went back to
test out another interloper. Good job, as I now exhibited yet
more dodgy behaviour. The FP coming in from the South
just W of ingerthorpe Hall was there clearly - but there was
only a faintly visible track N with a rope across the entrance
and a sign saying 'No Access' . After checking the map, I
ignored the warning and tried to pick up the route of the FP
as best I could. The local farmer has decided on expanding
the area of woodland on the map with a conifer plantation.
Past this, locations of previous walls have been moved. All
I had was the map and compass. Kept confidence in myself
and continued, finally finding the exact intersection with
Straight Lane complete with a FP sign (on the other side of
the stile).

A short section SE caused a disagreement with a true
rambler. A walker with bottle-bottomed glasses was coming
the other way and his first words were "are you lost?". Did
I look lost? He was more convinced when I told him I was
on the way to Ripon, and began to describe to me what I
should have done. In response to this I indicated that there
was more than one way of getting to Ripon, and explained
the route I was going to take. He then seemed irritated and
insisted that he would get there first!

Just after the little wood on the Left where this meeting
occurred, I spotted a mountain to climb which promised
to offer great views. On the map it was Marlow Hill, in the
middle of a muddy field. Decided to off-road and climbed
to the highest point, 122m with good views of the Vale of
York and the North York Moors.

Hurried back down to join the bridleway SW, then swung
N joining the Ripon Rowel Walk on its way up to Markenfield
Hall. It is a completely moated and fortified Manor House built
in 1310. "This wonderfully little-altered building is the most complete
surviving example of the medium-sized fourteenth century house
in England" - J R Robinson, The Architecture of Northern England .

I only got a glimpse of it through ugly farm buildings. Its was upon
to the public for a couple of week in May and June.

My nose for off-road next took me to the disused quarry E of
the Hall. This was an interesting place with some mostly
buried carved stone arches. Sat down for a coffee and in the
quiet could hear the sound of carols. Seemed to come from
the direction of fountains abbey. Reminded me of the off-road
experience at Abbott Hall.

Light was now fading as I followed the line of the Ripon Rowel
up towards Ripon, then followed the southern edge of Ripon
to meet up with the A61.
