Pike O'Blisco

26 Nov 2005

My second stab at an eXtreme lakes experience! This one a little wilder than the Kentmere camp. As we approached the weekend the weather forecast was littered with severe weather warnings and things looked pretty nasty.

Still we decided it was now or much later so went ahead with a slightly less ambitious plan of finding a campsite just off the floor or the Langdale valley. Plan A had been to drive up to Hardknott pass and camp half-way up Cold Pike.

The moment we came when I stepped out of the warm car at the head of Langdale and set my face to complete this challenge. I knew it was going to be a rough experience, but having steeled myself for this I felt some exhiliration as we strode off into the darkness in search of our site. Almost immediately, the will of Caradhras was set against us. The wind got up and hail was in our faces.

Strode through Stool End (lovely name) and past the farmer who simply ignored us and up towards "the Band". Ross's plan was to look for a campsite just above the little wooded area hear the intake wall. We skirted around the top of this area but found it to be fairly sloping. One area just inside the wood was flat but too rocky. So retraced our steps to a site we'd passed up previously as being a little sloping and settled for this as the best available option.

Up went the tent. I was not getting fairly cold with the wind chill and was glad to get in. The night was a repeat of the Kentmere camp with Ross sleeping soundly. I spent most of the night trying not to roll on top of him. In the middle of the night we were distured by a sheep who seemed equally disturbed that we were there. It was bleeting in a most distressed manner just outside the tent and seemed to be asking permission to join us inside. It finally gave up. Next morning there was a lone sheep lingering around the camp area - probably the same one!

The weather was no better the next day. In fact the rain was persistent throughout the entire period of the expedition! We decided against the Crinkles as we could see that snow was lying at high level. The days' target was Pike O' Blisco which was destined to become the first Wainwright to have been climbed three times. It reached that distinction throught the chance decisions of  others rather than the direction of my own will.

We'd just got going, reaching the FB crossing Oxendale Beck when we stopped for 20 minutes for the absolute necessity of taking water from the Beck, boiling it and storing it in a container. The 1.5 litres of water which I had was ample for both of  us in reality. He does take this ML stuff rather seriously at times.

There was an ongoing, unspoken tension between us in the matter of altitude readings which simmered away through the whole  time and came to a head on the summit! Ross had brought his new pride and joy - a flash (mechanical) barometer/altimeter! On numerous occasions he would study it and then state sagely that the pressure was doing this and that so the weather would do this and that at some unspecified time in the future. The altitude would also be read out with the confidence of one stating immutable fact. However this immutable fact didnt always get as close as my GPS to the OS MAP altitude. The acid test, I thought, would be what the two rivals would tell us on the summit of Pike O'Blisco, shown as 705m on the map.

We raced up the mountain. Ross seems to like treating climbing as aerobic exercise, pushing himself against the clock.My atitude is somewhat different. If I break out into a sweat, its time to look for an appropriately flat rock, sit down, and enjoy the view. The result of our contrasting approaches is the repeatedly Ross strikes out ahead, I plod along losing ground to him, he stops to wait for me then sets off again when I catch up. Groundhog day starring the Hare and the Tortoise.

Anyway, we reach the snowy summit. I reach for my GPS and, hardly able to contain my glee, annouce that it shows an altitude of 702, only 3 metres out. I dont get to hear the reading from the altimeter but do get treated to Ross's response- "So What!"

From this point on, the walk became eXtreme. The path off the summit we took headed in the direction of Bleaberry Knott  initially and took us, without option, over two rather nasty 10-12 rock climbing pitches. I've never had to do anything like this in the lakes before. I guess its a route that didnt even get into Wainwright. Clear route it was though. Add the falling snow, ice rocks and 30 mph winds and this was no walk-in-the-park. Ross went down the first pitch putting his rock climbing skills into practice and then guided me down. I didnt feel particularly comfortable, mostly because I was doing this with my pack on and felt it was pulling me off. I guess it puts the failure of Puig Caragoler to bed though. The next pitch was easier as I handed my rucksack to Ross before going down.

Things were back to sane lakeland standard after that. We yomped the rest of the path down Redacre Gill, pounding our joints on the horrible rock staircase. I must admit to really disliking these constructions. They are really hard work on the legs and give the walk a monotonous plodding sort of feeling.On my own I may have avoided the steps entirely and 'Off-Roaded'. We both abandoned them anyway after having slipped - ice had formed dangerously on them.

The challenge was over. Back into the air conditioned car. I'm more convinced than ever that camping and rain dont
go together. It was fun, but exausting. For me, not an experience to recharge batteries but rather to run them down.
