Dakar Academy to 56 Cite Al Amal, Dakar Senegal

28 October 2009

GPX file of similar walk on 13th May 2009

1. The front entrance to Ecole Maristes, the large catholic school. This taken when the mad rush for 8am had dissipated. Behind the photo, a taxi "rank" was situated where I would triumphantly walk past a taxi driver who knew I was walking to Patte D'oie. This was my favourite part of the day - 30 minutes to think and pray as I walked before arriving back at no56. It was too hot to do the walk to school at 2:30 until november.

2. Side street. The end of this street always had a security guard sat on the Right and another guy sat listening to the radio on the left. They were there at 8am and at 3pm, always the same!

3. The "scenic" part of the walk as I went along next to the inland lake. Suddenly all was very quiet, the noise of traffic had gone. A little further on, there was a korean family in a large house. The old lady of the house would often be sitting outside on a plastic chair. Next door in an undeveloped plot there was a wooden shack in which lived a man with two wives and numerous children.

4. Just past the Korean house, the road now passes under the line of pylons, also marking a line of undeveloped land used for cultivation. I would often see a labourer crossing the road with two full watering cans from the lake, taking the water the irrigate his little plot.

5. Passing under the line of pylons, I had to pass one of the neighbourhood rubbish tips. Assorted household waste would be dumped here and smell until someone (I know not who) came and burnt it all , releasing acrid fumes.

6. Some new rubbish (sifted for anything useful) and previous burnt rubbish to the left.

7. If only it worked!

8. Turning away from the inland lake towards Patte D'oie. The gouge in the middle of the road is evidence of the damage done to the roads by the rains

9. Walking alongside the second inland lake. Further up at a crossroads, a disabled woman was already on duty in her wheelchair. Each day she was there and by her constancy her presence and need slowly grew in my consciousness. This how she would build up over time a list of "clients" who would know where to find here if they felt the need to give alms.

10. Going up the "steps" to the autoroute. This was fun the day it rained a couple of weeks before. Next to this, there was a street barbers shop with a hand-painted alien who had received a cut there. This bit of the walk was officially illegal, and twice I had to take a longer route to go over the passerelle when there was a traffic policeman here.

11. Crossing over the autoroute. This is the newer section leading to the payage in contruction.

12. Entering the allotments area after the autoroute was crossed. The path leading through was covered in detritus but to either side there was green. A well was just ahead, often with a horse and cart parked while the horse drinks some fetid water. Just here there was also a place where clay pots were molded in the ground before being taken out to dry in the sun.

13.

14. Finally at the end of the alottments, Cite Al Amal comes into view. First I must cross the local rubbish tip. The cause of the acrid fumes which often bothered Linda. Another fire is burning on the left. Just before entering the cite, there was a shack shop advertising "deratisation"

15. Walking up towards the mosque. On the left just before this was the building site where the more breathing space was disappearing forever. Each day I would note progress. Then a left turn towards home.
